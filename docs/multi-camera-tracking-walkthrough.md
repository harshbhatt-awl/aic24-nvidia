# Multi-Camera People Tracking — Walkthrough of the Pipeline and Recent Improvements

This document walks through how our multi-camera people-tracking pipeline works on the NVIDIA Warehouse scene and what we changed over the last session to raise its accuracy. It is written for someone familiar with computer vision basics but not necessarily with the specific multi-camera tracking literature, so the geometry and the tracker concepts are explained from the ground up before the parameter changes are discussed.

## The setup we are tracking

The scene is a warehouse covered by seven fixed cameras. The cameras are mounted at different heights and orientations: four of them give relatively clean side-on views of the floor (cameras 0390 through 0393), while the other three (0394, 0395, 0396) are mounted toward the back of the warehouse at more oblique or overhead angles. Twenty-five people walk around the warehouse over a thirty-second clip, sometimes visible to several cameras at once, sometimes only to one. The job of the pipeline is to produce, for every frame, the 2D image-plane bounding boxes of every visible person on every camera, the consistent identity of each person across cameras, and the (x, y) position of each person on the warehouse floor in metres. Tracking quality is measured by a metric called HOTA, with separate scores for image-plane accuracy and 3D world-coordinate accuracy.

## What camera calibration gives us

Each of the seven cameras has been calibrated by the dataset organizers, which means we know two things about it. The first is the intrinsic matrix, usually called K, which describes the lens itself — the focal length, the optical centre, and how 3D rays from the world get projected onto the 2D image sensor. The second is the extrinsic data, R and t, which describes where the camera is positioned in the warehouse and which direction it is facing. R is a 3-by-3 rotation matrix and t is the translation vector that, together, tell you how to transform points from the world's coordinate frame into the camera's coordinate frame. With K, R, and t in hand, the full camera projection matrix P is a 3-by-4 matrix that takes any 3D point in the warehouse and tells you exactly which pixel that point would land on in the camera's image. This is the mathematical backbone of everything that follows — without good calibration, no amount of clever tracking can recover 3D positions reliably.

## The homography, and why we use it

The full camera matrix P maps from 3D world to 2D image, but we frequently want to go the other way: given a 2D pixel on the image, where is that point on the warehouse floor? In general this is under-determined — a single pixel corresponds to an entire ray of 3D points, not one. But there is a special case that rescues us. If we know that the 2D point we care about lies on the ground plane (Z=0 in the warehouse coordinate system), then the projection from "world point on the floor" to "image pixel" simplifies to a 3-by-3 matrix called the homography. Concretely, you take the camera's full projection matrix and drop the third column (the column that handles Z), and what remains maps any (x, y) on the floor to its pixel location. Inverting this homography gives you exactly what you need: given a pixel that you believe corresponds to a point on the floor, you get back the (x, y) coordinate of that point in metres. Every camera has its own homography because every camera sees the floor from a different position and angle. The seven homography matrices are what let us take a 2D detection on any camera and place that person on a shared warehouse floor map.

## How the pipeline chains these pieces together

The pipeline runs in eight stages, but the relevant flow is short. First, a person detector (YOLO11 in our case) looks at every video frame from every camera and outputs the bounding boxes of every person it sees. Then a re-identification model (SOLIDER) processes each detected person crop and produces a 768-dimensional embedding vector that should be similar for the same person and different for different people, regardless of which camera saw them. A pose model (RTMPose) also runs on each detection and produces seventeen body keypoints — head, shoulders, hips, knees, ankles, and so on. After this, two trackers run. The single-camera tracker takes the per-camera detections and links them into tracks within each camera based on the ReID embedding similarity. The multi-camera tracker then takes those per-camera tracks and clusters them across cameras into globally-consistent identities by matching their ReID embeddings AND by checking that their world coordinates agree closely enough. Finally an evaluation stage measures HOTA, IDF1, MOTA and similar metrics against the ground truth.

The crucial step in this chain is the moment when each 2D detection box gets converted into a world coordinate. The tracker uses a single pixel from each bounding box, applies the camera's homography, and reads off the (x, y) world position. The choice of which pixel to use is what determines whether the world coordinates are accurate. If we pick the wrong pixel — say, a pixel that is not actually on the floor — the homography gives us a wildly wrong world position, because the homography assumes the input pixel corresponds to a point on the ground plane. Get this single choice right and the multi-camera tracker has good data to work with. Get it wrong and the rest of the pipeline cannot recover, no matter how good the ReID embeddings are.

## What the parameter tuning changed

The first improvement we landed was in this very choice of pixel. The legacy pipeline used the bottom-centre of each detection bounding box as the world-projection point, on the assumption that the bottom of a person's bounding box should sit at floor level. That works in textbook diagrams but falls apart in practice — detection boxes are noisy, they often include shadow regions or extend below the actual feet, and they tilt when the person is moving fast. We replaced bbox-bottom with the pose model's ankle keypoints, which are pixel-accurate locations of where the model thinks each foot is. The instinct here is to average both ankles to get a single representative foot point, and that is the choice we tried first; but it turned out to be worse than the bbox-bottom we started with, by about 0.03 in world HOTA. The reason is that during a normal walking stride, one foot is in the air. The midpoint of two ankles, when one of them is airborne, sits noticeably above the floor. Because the homography is built on the assumption that the input pixel is on the ground plane, feeding it an above-ground point places the resulting world coordinate not just slightly off, but far off — it projects the point along the camera ray to wherever that ray hits the floor, which can be metres away from where the person is actually standing. The right answer is to pick the lower of the two ankles, meaning the one with the larger pixel-y coordinate (in image coordinates, larger y means further down the image, closer to the bottom), which is the foot currently planted on the floor. With this change alone, world HOTA went from 0.4583 to 0.4932, a gain of 0.035 with no other changes to the pipeline.

The second improvement addressed temporal noise. Even with the correct ankle keypoint, the projected world position bounces around frame to frame because of small pixel-level errors in the pose estimate, slight sway as a person walks, and brief occlusions. The natural fix is temporal smoothing, and we used a simple exponential moving average — for each person and each frame, the smoothed world position is thirty percent of the current observation plus seventy percent of the previous smoothed value. This is cheap to compute, has no special-case logic, and reduces high-frequency jitter without introducing the lag that you would get from a wider smoothing window. We swept the smoothing weight across several values and found that 0.3 is the optimum: any heavier (a weight of 0.1 means ninety percent of the smoothed value carries forward each frame) and the smoother starts attenuating real motion below baseline; any lighter and there is too little noise reduction to matter. With smoothing on top of ankle-lower projection, world HOTA reached 0.5055 — a combined gain of 0.047 over the legacy bbox-bottom setup.

The third improvement was a tracker re-tuning made possible by the cleaner world coordinates. The single-camera tracker has a parameter called epsilon_scpt that controls how aggressively it merges nearby detections into a single track. When this value is low, the tracker fragments tracks into many short ones; when it is high, it merges aggressively and risks merging two different people who happened to walk close together. The previous tuning had set it to 0.30, which was the best value found when the world coordinates were noisy — when world coordinates are unreliable, conservative clustering is the safer choice. With our cleaner ankle-projected and smoothed world coordinates, the tracker can rely on those positions more confidently and can cluster more aggressively without false merges. A fresh sweep of values from 0.10 to 0.50 confirmed this, and showed that the optimum had shifted to 0.20. Promoting this value into the baseline gave us another gain of 0.023 in world HOTA and, importantly, 0.022 in image HOTA as well — the image-side gain is what convinces us that this is a genuine tracking improvement and not just a metric artifact, because the image HOTA does not depend on world projection at all.

## Where we ended up

The cumulative effect of these three changes is documented below, comparing the baseline at the start of the session to the current state. The pipeline architecture is unchanged; the model checkpoints are unchanged; what shipped is a different way of computing world coordinates and a single retuned tracker threshold.

| Metric | Starting baseline | After ankle + smoothing | After tracker re-tune (current) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Image HOTA | 0.7580 | 0.7580 | 0.7800 |
| Image IDF1 | 0.7495 | 0.7495 | 0.7670 |
| Image MOTA | 0.7806 | 0.7806 | 0.7888 |
| World HOTA | 0.4583 | 0.5055 | 0.5282 |
| World AssA | 0.5297 | 0.5946 | 0.6338 |
| World IDF1 | 0.6475 | 0.6587 | 0.6782 |

The headline number is the world HOTA going from 0.4583 to 0.5282, which is a 15.3 percent relative improvement. Equally important is that the AssA component — which measures how well the tracker associates the same person across time and cameras — moved by more than ten percentage points, while the DetA component (how well it detects people in the first place) moved much less. This is exactly the pattern we wanted to see, because the changes targeted the cross-camera matching step that lives downstream of detection.

## What is still in the way of further improvement

The largest remaining bottleneck has nothing to do with the changes we made and lives instead in the detector and ReID stages. On three of the seven cameras — 0394, 0395, and 0396 — the YOLO detector finds substantially fewer people than are actually visible according to the ground truth. Camera 0396 in particular has detection recall of only thirty-five percent, meaning two out of three people visible to that camera are simply missed. Even when detections are found and clustered into per-camera tracks, the multi-camera tracker fails to link those back-camera tracks with the front-camera tracks because the ReID embeddings from those oblique viewpoints sit too far apart in feature space. The result is that camera 0396 contributes zero people to the final 3D world tracks, even though there is good ground-truth data for it. Two follow-up workstreams are scoped for these problems: a detector-recall investigation that will sweep the detection confidence threshold to see whether the detector is genuinely blind to those views or just filtering valid detections too aggressively, and a ReID fine-tuning workstream that adapts the SOLIDER backbone to warehouse camera angles by training in Colab on additional warehouse data. Both designs are committed to the repository, in `docs/superpowers/specs/`, and either can be picked up in a future session.

The smaller follow-ups are a pair of diagnostics surfaced by the tracker sweep. Two of the tracker parameters — `hard_world_gate` and `sim_th` — turned out to have no measurable effect when their values were varied across the expected range. This either means the parameters are not actually wired into the tracker (a code bug) or that their thresholds are not binding in this scene (a data property, possibly tied to the back-camera issue). Either way they are worth a short investigation before any further tuning work.
